Yokohama Onsen Hotel: Staying at Minatomirai Manyo-Club

April 2026  ·  Minatomirai, Yokohama  ·  By HotelManga Editorial

Yokohama Minatomirai Manyo-Club is one of Japan's most extraordinary urban onsen experiences — a place where you can soak in genuine hot spring water from Atami and Yugawara while watching the Cosmo World Ferris wheel glow against the Yokohama night sky. It is not a hotel in the conventional sense. It is an eight-story sanctuary of warmth, yukata, manga libraries, foot massage chairs, rooftop footbaths, and real mineral-rich waters that have been arriving daily by tanker truck for decades. This complete guide covers the onsen facilities, room types, rooftop footbath, dining, how to get there, and everything you need to plan your stay.

You are stepping off the Minatomirai Line when the waterfront opens in front of you — Yokohama Bay Bridge in the distance, the Cosmo Clock 21 Ferris wheel turning slowly against the skyline, and directly ahead, a building that smells faintly of sulfur and cedar in the best possible way. You are five minutes from the station and already somewhere else entirely.

What Makes Manyo-Club Different From a Regular Onsen

Most urban onsen facilities use tap water heated to temperature and flavored with mineral additives. Manyo-Club does not. The hot spring water used in every bath, indoor and outdoor, is transported from the famous hot spring sources of Atami and Yugawara — two of Japan's most historically celebrated onsen towns — by a fleet of 20,000-liter tanker trucks making the journey six times a day. This practice is rooted in an Edo-period tradition called okumi-yu, in which wealthy patrons and shoguns had quality hot spring water brought to them rather than making the journey themselves. At Manyo-Club, that tradition has scaled into something that serves thousands of guests daily, without compromising the quality of what flows into the tubs.

The water from Yugawara in particular is colorless, odorless, and silky-smooth against the skin — the kind of water that leaves you convinced something genuinely therapeutic has happened. Guests consistently report that their skin feels noticeably different after an extended soak. This is not marketing language. It is what happens when you bathe in properly sourced, naturally mineral-rich water rather than its urban approximation.

The result is a facility that occupies a rare middle ground: the authenticity of a traditional onsen resort, combined with the convenience of a Yokohama waterfront address five minutes from a major transit hub. It is open 24 hours a day. It offers overnight accommodation. And it attracts everyone from day-tripping locals to international travelers who have specifically made the journey from Tokyo to soak here.

The Onsen Baths: What's Available

The main bathing floors of Manyo-Club are separated by gender, as is standard in Japanese onsen facilities. What distinguishes this property is the sheer variety of bathing environments available within a single building. The facility features thirteen natural hot spring baths in total — three outdoor and three indoor baths for men, four outdoor and three indoor baths for women — alongside specialized bathing environments that most onsen facilities do not offer at all.

The cypress bath (hinoki-buro) is warm-scented and traditional, exactly the kind of tub you imagine when you picture a Japanese hot spring experience. The energy bath circulates the water for a gentle hydrotherapy effect. There is a large indoor bath designed for long, meditative soaking — ideal in cold weather when the outdoor baths feel bracing between dips. For women, a Nanocla room offers a nano-mist and micro-cloud steam treatment that has become one of the most talked-about features on review platforms, particularly among guests who make repeat visits.

The ganbanyoku — bedrock baths, where guests lie on warm mineral stone slabs heated from below — are available for an additional fee and are divided into four types by purpose: relaxation, detox, circulation, and warming. These are the rooms where guests typically fall asleep, which is considered entirely normal and socially acceptable. The herbal sauna and salt sauna rounds out the thermal options. Between bath types, the temperature changes and the sensations vary enough to fill an entire day without repeating yourself.

For guests with tattoos: the public bathing areas maintain traditional policies. Small tattoos (under 11cm) can be covered with a provided seal at a nominal cost, but larger tattoos restrict access to the communal baths. Private family baths are available for reservation and offer a guaranteed, fully private onsen experience — the recommended option for tattooed guests and for families with young children who prefer not to bathe communally.

The Rooftop Observatory Footbath: The View That Makes Guests Stay Longer

If there is a single feature that distinguishes Manyo-Club from any other onsen facility in the greater Tokyo area, it is the rooftop Observatory Footbath Garden — a roughly 50-meter-circumference outdoor footbath that wraps around the top of the building with panoramic views of the Minatomirai 21 district in every direction.

Standing in the footbath at dusk, with warm spring water rising around your ankles, you are looking directly at the Cosmo Clock 21 Ferris wheel as it begins its illuminated evening rotation. To the right, the Yokohama Bay Bridge arcs across the water. On clear days — most common in autumn and winter — Mount Fuji appears on the western horizon, framed between the towers of Minato Mirai. At night, the reflections of the city lights shimmer in the footbath water below your feet. This is the view that guests consistently photograph and consistently struggle to describe to people who haven't seen it.

The footbath is accessible wearing yukata — the comfortable Japanese garment provided to all guests — which means you can move directly from a soaking bath to the rooftop without changing clothes or making elaborate preparations. Many guests spend their evenings rotating between the indoor baths and the footbath terrace, timing their visits to catch the Ferris wheel's changing light patterns after dark. There is no better place at Manyo-Club to sit with a drink and let the day dissolve.

Rooms: Japanese Style, Western Style, and the Tatami Experience

Manyo-Club offers 94 guest rooms across Japanese-style and Western-style configurations. The property is fundamentally an onsen facility first and a hotel second — which means rooms are sized accordingly: compact, efficiently designed, and equipped for exactly what you need when the main event is happening on the bathing floors, not in your room.

The Japanese-style rooms are the ones worth choosing if you have not slept on a tatami floor before. They feature woven-straw flooring, low tables, and futon mattresses laid directly on the tatami — a firmer sleeping surface than a Western bed, and one that a meaningful number of guests either love immediately or take one night to adjust to. All rooms include a TV, refrigerator, electric kettle, green tea, mineral water, and high-tech toilet — the standard inventory of a quality Japanese property. Skin toner, facial cleanser, and skin lotion are provided in the bathroom, which reflects the bathing culture the facility is built around.

Western-style rooms add a reading light and replace the futon arrangement with a conventional bed. Views from upper-floor rooms facing the waterfront are spectacular — particularly the rooms that look directly toward the bay and the Ferris wheel. Multiple guests across review platforms specifically call out the room views as a highlight, with one reviewer on Booking.com noting that the view more than compensated for the compact room size. If views matter to you, request a waterfront-facing room on a high floor when booking.

One practical point worth noting honestly: the check-in and check-out process is more elaborate than a standard hotel, requiring guests to navigate between the reception floor and their room via a locker system with a barcode key. Several reviews describe this as a mild inconvenience — particularly for guests unfamiliar with Japanese onsen protocol. It is worth reading the property's guest instructions before arrival so the process feels familiar rather than confusing when you get there.

Dining at Manyo-Club

The facility has two primary dining areas. Manyo-an is a semi-private restaurant styled in the manner of a traditional Japanese dining room, serving Japanese course meals including sashimi, tempura, udon, and soba. It is the higher-end option and the one to choose for a relaxed, extended dinner in yukata after an evening in the baths. The Manyo Gozen course — a set menu of sashimi, tempura, and noodles — is the most frequently ordered item and receives consistently positive comments.

Ikoi-dokoro (the "Resting Place") operates 24 hours a day and functions as a food court with a broader menu spanning Japanese, Western, and Chinese cuisine. Guests describe it as somewhere between a family restaurant and a hotel buffet — functional, reasonably priced, and genuinely useful at 2 AM when hunger strikes between late-night baths. For overnight guests, the morning meal is a breakfast buffet included in the accommodation rate, and it is well-regarded: multiple reviews specifically praise the sushi and oysters available at breakfast, which is a combination that tends to make early rising feel worthwhile.

Beyond the two restaurants, there is a fruit juice and smoothie bar featuring Cold Stone ice cream — a counterintuitive but extremely popular post-bath snack — and a bar specializing in Kirin beer. The Kirin bar closes earlier than the onsen itself, which is worth noting if a late evening beer is part of your post-soak ritual.

For entertainment beyond the baths and meals, Manyo-Club offers a game center with air hockey, table tennis, karaoke rooms, a kids' play area, and — in a detail that will resonate with HotelManga readers — a manga comic book reading area stocked with titles ranging from current releases to classic back catalog. There are also two relaxation rooms with reclining chairs and TVs (one women-only) where guests frequently nap between onsen sessions. The building is genuinely designed for spending an entire day inside it without running out of things to do.

Location: Yokohama's Waterfront Entertainment District

Manyo-Club sits in the Minatomirai 21 district — Yokohama's futuristic waterfront development, built on reclaimed land that was once the city's main industrial port. The building's address is 2-7-1 Shinko, Naka Ward — which places it immediately adjacent to the Cup Noodles Museum (two minutes on foot), Yokohama Cosmo World amusement park (directly in front), and the Red Brick Warehouse shopping and events complex (nine minutes on foot along the waterfront promenade).

For visitors who want to see more of Yokohama before or after their onsen experience, Yamashita Park — the iconic seaside park on Yokohama's historic bay — is about 20 minutes on foot or a short taxi ride. Yokohama Chinatown, one of the largest in Asia, is a similar distance. The Yokohama Landmark Tower Sky Garden — offering some of the highest views of the Yokohama skyline — is walkable from the property in under 15 minutes.

For Tokyo day-trippers who have never made the 30-minute journey to Yokohama: the combination of Manyo-Club's onsen, the waterfront views, the Red Brick Warehouse, and a walk through Chinatown makes for one of the most satisfying day trips from the capital — and one that most visitors to Japan miss entirely by staying in Shinjuku or Shibuya. The Minatomirai district has its own aesthetic identity, less frantic and more deliberately designed than central Tokyo, and Manyo-Club is its most distinctive lodging option.

Getting to Manyo-Club

The property is exceptionally well-connected from both the Yokohama station network and directly from the airports. For international visitors arriving at Haneda — which handles most domestic and short-haul international flights — the journey is particularly direct.

From Minatomirai Station

  • Exit → 5-minute walk to the facility
  • Minatomirai Line (from Yokohama Station)
  • Signposted toward the waterfront
  • The Ferris wheel is visible as a landmark

From Sakuragicho Station

  • JR Keihin-Tohoku Negishi Line
  • Yokohama Municipal Subway Blue Line
  • 15-minute walk via Kishamichi Promenade
  • Scenic waterfront route recommended

From Haneda Airport

  • Limousine Bus: stop is 1-minute walk from entrance
  • Train via Keikyu Line to Yokohama: ~30 min
  • Taxi: approximately ¥5,000–¥7,000
  • Closest airport to the property by far

Free Shuttle from Yokohama Station

  • Departs from Yokohama Station West Exit
  • Free shuttle bus for guests and visitors
  • Check Manyo-Club's official schedule for times
  • Useful with luggage or in bad weather

Self-parking is available at the property. The free shuttle service from Yokohama Station West Exit is a particularly useful option for guests arriving with luggage, since navigating the Minatomirai district on foot with a full suitcase requires navigating several elevated walkways. For most visitors arriving by train, the Minatomirai Station exit is the cleaner option — and the five-minute walk along the waterfront is pleasant enough to count as the start of the experience rather than a prelude to it.

Cleanliness: What Guests Actually Report

Cleanliness is overwhelmingly positive across review platforms for Manyo-Club, and this is perhaps unsurprising for a facility whose entire identity is built around bathing. The locker areas, bathing floors, and shared spaces are cleaned on a continuous rotation. The women's vanity area — which multiple guests describe in detail — is equipped with Shiseido toiletries in full-size bottles, individual hair dryers, toners, and a makeup station that several reviewers mention specifically and positively. The standard of amenities in the changing areas is markedly above what you would find at a typical public bath.

Room cleanliness receives consistently strong marks, with guests noting that tatami rooms arrive fresh and that the high-tech toilets are immaculate. The one dimension that draws occasional criticism is noise — the air conditioning units in some rooms are described as loud, with at least one reviewer recommending that light sleepers switch them off at night. This is worth noting if you are a light sleeper, and worth asking about a quieter room when booking.

Value: Is Manyo-Club Worth the Price?

The question of value at Manyo-Club requires separating the day-visit experience from the overnight stay. For day visitors, the admission fee of approximately ¥2,950 for adults covers unlimited access to all onsen baths, the rooftop footbath, relaxation rooms, manga library, and game center — from 10:00 AM until 9:00 AM the following morning if you wish. At that price, it is one of the best-value full-day experiences in greater Yokohama, and multiple reviewers use words like "inexpensive" and "great value" relative to the breadth of what is included.

For overnight guests, the room rate includes admission, a late-night fee if you use the facilities after 3:00 AM, and a breakfast buffet. At approximately ¥10,000–¥15,000 per person per night depending on room type and season, the all-in calculation becomes genuinely favorable when you factor in what a comparable onsen day pass alone would cost at a traditional ryokan outside the city. The breakfast buffet — which includes sushi and oysters — is widely considered a meaningful part of the value proposition rather than an afterthought.

One critical practical note: the property accepts cash only for in-facility payments during your stay, though credit cards are accepted at check-in. An accommodation tax is charged separately and paid at the property. Plan accordingly and arrive with sufficient yen, as the nearest ATM options may require a short walk.

The rooms themselves are compact — this is the most consistent criticism across platforms. Guests who approach Manyo-Club as a conventional hotel and measure the room against the price will frequently feel underwhelmed. Guests who understand they are paying primarily for the onsen experience, the views, and the overall atmosphere will almost universally leave satisfied. The distinction matters, and it is worth being honest with yourself about which category you fall into before booking.

The Manga Library: Reading Spot for Otaku Guests

Tucked into the entertainment floor of the building — the same level as the game center, kids' corner, and table tennis — is the facility's manga reading area, known internally as the Dokusho-dokoro (読書処, or "Reading Spot"). For a property that positions itself primarily as an onsen experience, the manga collection is a genuine and well-curated surprise.

The collection at the Yokohama Minatomirai location runs to approximately 1,000 volumes — a figure that places it meaningfully above a token shelf but below the sprawling multi-thousand-volume archives found at dedicated manga cafés in Akihabara or Shibuya. The selection spans classic titles through recent releases, with a lineup that Japanese review sites describe as including nostalgic back-catalog works alongside currently running series — meaning that both the guest who wants to reread a beloved older title and the one who wants to catch up on a current arc will find something. The curation leans toward titles recognizable across age groups, avoiding the hyperspecialized sectioning of a dedicated maid or otaku café in favor of a broader appeal.

The reading area is available to all guests as part of standard admission — there is no additional charge to access the manga, unlike the ganbanyoku rooms or private baths which carry separate fees. Guests typically arrive in yukata directly from the baths, select a volume from the shelves, and settle into one of the reclining chairs in the adjacent relaxation rooms. This combination — warm from a mineral soak, yukata-clad, manga in hand, Yokohama waterfront visible through the windows — is precisely what earns the property its Manga Sleepover designation. For manga readers who have always wanted to read their favorite series in an authentic Japanese onsen setting, this is a rare opportunity to do exactly that without leaving the building.

The manga area is not staffed separately and operates on the trust system standard to Japanese facilities: take a volume, read it, return it to the shelf. Photography is not permitted on the bathing floors, but the reading corner is generally photographable — and the rows of volumes in the relaxation area regularly appear in guest photos on review platforms. The collection is in Japanese only; the facility does not currently stock titles in English, Chinese, or Korean, which is worth noting for international guests who cannot read Japanese manga in the original.

Tips for Getting the Most Out of Your Stay

Book a waterfront-facing room on a high floor if the views matter to you — they do, noticeably. The difference between a bay-facing room and an interior-facing room is the difference between waking up to the Yokohama skyline and waking up to a courtyard. It is worth specifying this preference in your booking request.

Plan your rooftop footbath visit for dusk. The transition from daylight to the illuminated Ferris wheel and city lights takes about 20 minutes and is the single most photographed moment at the property. Being in the footbath as that transition happens — rather than arriving after dark — is the version worth timing.

Pick a yukata pattern early. Female guests can choose from nine different yukata designs at check-in, and the more popular patterns go quickly during busy periods. This is a small detail, but guests who mention it tend to mention it fondly — it is one of several moments at Manyo-Club where the experience feels more considered than you might expect from a property at this price point.

If you are visiting on a day trip without an overnight stay, arrive when the facility opens at 10:00 AM and treat the first hour as your own — the crowds build significantly through the afternoon and peak on weekend evenings. The morning baths, with steam rising off the water and the Yokohama waterfront coming to life outside the windows, are an experience that many repeat visitors specifically time their visits around.
